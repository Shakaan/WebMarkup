An introduction to Haarlem

This classic Dutch city is known for it's historic buildings, grand churches, museums, bars, fine cafes and it's cobbled streets. Some recommended places to visit would be the stadsbibliotheek which is the Public Library in Haarlem, theater De Toneelschuur, the Ten Boom Museum, Sint-Bavo Cathedral, and the Haarlemmerhout park.

In conclusion, there are a lot of things to do and discover in Haarlem.
A history of great artists

During the Golden age, the Haarlem council decided to promote the pursuit of arts and history, in an attempt to better the economy. Many artists and craftsmen migrated to Haarlem and received commissions from the Haarlem council to decorate the city hall. The paintings commissioned were meant to show Haarlem's glorious history as well as Haarlem's glorious products. Famous painters, such as Frans Hals and Jacob van Ruisdael, contributed heavily to this.

The art and architecture makes Haarlem a perfect place for tourists interessted in culture and history.
Haarlem Center

There is a lot to do in Haarlem Center. In this section, we will explore the various shops, companies, and touristic attractions there are in the neighbourhood.

In the City Center lies the Grote Markt, which houses a number of special historic buildings, including the town hall, the Vleeshal and the St. Bavokerk. The St. Bavokerk is a church with its tower almost reaching up to 80 meters. Many famous musicians, including Mozart, have been recorded to have played there. There is also the school building of the Stedelijk Gymnasium Haarlem, one of the earliest schools. In addition, there is a market twice a week on the Grote Markt and various events take place throughout the year, including the annual fair.

Important shopping streets run from Grote Markt in three directions, namely Grote Houtstraat, Barteljorisstraat and Zijlstraat. There are a number of small, characteristic shopping streets in the historic center, such as Warmoesstraat, Schagchelstraat, and Kleine Houtstraat.

The Spaarne Rivier devides the city center into two parts. West of the river is the largest part of the city center. To the east is the part also known as the Spaarnwouderbuurt. There, you will find among other things, the only remaining city gate of Haarlem; The Amsterdamse Poort. The mill De Adriaan and Koepelgevangenis are also located here. Both banks of the Spaarne are connected by two monumental swing bridges, the Catherijnebrug and the Melkbrug and two drawbridges including the Gravestenenbrug, and a modern drawbridge. This brudge, popularly called the 'Verfroller', is officially called the Long Bridge. In the past there was also the Sint Nicolaas or Coral bridge between the Houtmarkt and the Koudehorn, but this has disappeared. There have been plans to bring this bridge back, but no action has been takes as of today.

Many of the buildings in the city center date from the 16th, 17th and 19th centuries. However, there is also a lot of modern architecture, such as the Toneelschuur and the new building around the Appelaar.

In conclusion, there is a lot of cultural history to be found in Haarlem Center. There are also many shops to visit, and a lot of events are held there annualy.
